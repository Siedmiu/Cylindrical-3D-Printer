# Chapter 5: Literature Review Abstract

This chapter evaluates the current state of FDM 3D printing, focusing specifically on the limitations of traditional Cartesian architectures and the emergent potential of non-planar, particularly cylindrical, printing technologies. The review identifies key geometric and mechanical constraints in conventional FDM, such as the obligatory reliance on support structures for complex or overhanging geometries, which prolongs manufacturing time, increases material waste, and introduces pronounced mechanical anisotropy due to weak interlayer adhesion. 

To bypass these challenges, research towards non-planar printing proposes utilizing printers with non-Cartesian architectures or increased degrees of freedom (DOF). Cylindrical printers, representing a simple yet effective modification of replacing the linear y-axis with a rotary drum, offer the ability to produce support-free geometries like propellers, spiral constructs, and turning-like parts. The chapter highlights the particular relevance of this technology to the bioprinting sector, where radially outward, layer-by-layer printing prevents the structural collapse of fragile tubular tissues (such as blood vessels) and stents.

Despite successful prototypes introduced over the past decade, the broader adoption of additive lathe (AL) technology is obstructed by a significant research gap: the absence of comprehensive slicing software capable of planning full tool paths for curved surfaces. To date, researchers have had to rely on cumbersome workarounds or proprietary adaptations with heavy geometric limitations. The review also touches on the mechanical necessity of dedicated drive reductions for rotary axes to prevent vertical fine artifacts (VFAs) and maintain positional resolution in cylindrical configurations.

This chapter introduces the motivation and context for developing a cylindrical 3D printer, highlighting the evolution of FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling) technology from rapid prototyping to mature additive manufacturing. The primary goal of the thesis is to design and build a standalone cylindrical 3D printer prototype from scratch, moving away from conventional Cartesian adaptations to fully leverage cylindrical geometry. Along with the mechanical construction, the research focuses on developing specialized toolpath planning algorithms.

The scope of the project purposefully relies on accessible, open-source rep-rap components and FDM-printable parts to ensure reproducibility. While acknowledging certain limitations like the lack of a closed-loop feedback or a dedicated HMI, the prototype serves to demonstrate manufacturing capabilities unattainable on standard Cartesian printers. Finally, the chapter outlines the structure of the thesis, detailing the progression from literature review and mathematical foundations to system architecture, design principles, and slicing algorithm development.

# Chapter 3 Abstract: System Design and Architecture

This chapter details the mechanical, kinematic, electronic, and software design of the cylindrical 3D printer prototype (AL). 

- **Mechanical Design**: The structure relies on cost-effective, open-source compatible aluminum extrusions and 3D-printed components. A key material choice included PC/ABS for heat-adjacent parts due to its thermal resistance, alongside PLA for other structural components.
- **Kinematics**: The system adapts a standard Cartesian framework into polar kinematics by replacing the transverse linear Y-axis with a continuous rotary drum. To achieve high angular resolution while minimizing mechanical backlash, various reduction mechanisms were tested, culminating in a successful two-stage belt drive offering a 16:1 gear ratio. A custom printhead was also designed to adjust the nozzle's alignment with the drum's rotary axis.
- **Electronics & Control**: The printer is powered by a 32-bit BTT SKR V1.4 Turbo board paired with TMC2208 stepper drivers operating in UART and StealthChop modes. Temperature regulation is handled via a PID-controlled cartridge heater and NTC 100k thermistor.
- **Marlin Firmware Implementation**: The open-source Marlin firmware was customized to interpret polar coordinates by enabling the POLAR module. Adjustments included removing mechanical homing limits for the continuous rotary axis, remapping hardware pins for appropriate fan control, and executing calibration procedures to fine-tune steps per unit and PID thermal parameters for optimal performance.

# Abstract: Manufacturing Capabilities and Applications

This chapter explores the manufacturing capabilities and constraints associated with Forward Deposition Modeling (FDM) technology, centering around Design for Additive Manufacturing (DfAM) principles. Initially, it examines Cartesian 3D printing, evaluating how critical parameters like build orientation, raster angle, layer thickness, and infill impact the structural strength, mechanical durability, and surface texture of printed parts. Limitations such as weak interlayer bonds and the necessity of support structures for overhanging geometries are also addressed.

The chapter then extends these DfAM principles to cylindrical 3D printing, detailing its operational paradigm and radial manufacturing direction. It highlights unique challenges, such as the potential for delamination when depositing parallel helical paths, and introduces pattern arrangements like face-centered cubic (FCC) lattices to mitigate these structural weaknesses.

Finally, the text underscores the distinct geometric advantages of cylindrical 3D printing over traditional Cartesian methods. Key benefits include the continuity of rotation for seamless structural properties, the capability to manufacture complex internal geometries layer by layer, and most notably, the ability to eliminate support structures for complex overhangs—exemplified by a propeller design—thereby drastically reducing printing times and material consumption.

# Chapter 5: Path Generation

This chapter explores various methods for generating tool paths for cylindrical 3D printers. It first examines the application of existing Cartesian slicers, proposing a geometry transformation algorithm that compensates for cylindrical distortions alongside a script for recalculating extrusion parameters. This approach yields good dimensional accuracy but lacks topological continuity for fully rotational objects. An alternative methodology using algorithmic G-code generation via FullControl Designer is discussed, focusing on its ability to produce complex parametric paths natively in cylindrical coordinates without the discontinuities created by traditional slicers. A custom cylindrical slicer implemented within FullControl, inspired by recent research, is also presented; it extracts print contours directly onto concentric cylindrical layers. Although the custom slicer successfully mapped paths with rectilinear infill for simple geometries, it faced computational bottlenecks and contour generation errors when processing highly complex models. The chapter suggests further optimizations in contour extraction as a vital future step.

# Chapter 6: Results and Discussion - Abstract

This chapter summarizes the results and discussion concerning the design and toolpath generation for a cylindrical printer. It highlights the success of mechanical reduction and belt drives in achieving optimal resolution. The adaptation of the Marlin software for polar kinematics via the POLAR module proved successful, identifying a need for bed-to-nozzle calibration and a drum centering mechanism. The chapter also evaluates three toolpath generation methods: mapping Cartesian slices (best for complex shapes but lacks continuous rotation), expanding the FullControl GCode Designer (offers full control but is tedious for each segment), and a dedicated prototype cylindrical slicer (optimal mechanically but struggles with complex geometry). Finally, it outlines future development directions, emphasizing the critical need for robust contour extraction, advanced partial infill strategies accounting for cylindrical curvature, and modern print optimizations like pressure control and input shaping.
